Overview

Building Energy Management Systems (BEMS) require accurate load prediction to optimize HVAC control, ESS operation, and demand response, ultimately reducing energy cost and peak load.

This project develops a forecasting pipeline and evaluates how well machine learning models capture hourly and daily demand patterns.

Dataset & Key Observation

The dataset contains hourly electricity demand with weather variables (e.g., temperature) and calendar/time information.

Exploratory analysis shows that temperature alone explains less than half of demand variation (R² ≈ 0.42), indicating that time/behavioral patterns are essential.

Why Temperature Alone Is Not Enough

Seasonal effects can increase load both in summer (cooling) and winter (heating), meaning a single linear relationship is insufficient.

Methodology

Workflow: data loading → preprocessing → feature engineering → model training → testing → visualization.

Time-series features and engineered variables help the model learn daily/weekly cycles and short-term dependencies.

Code Architecture

The implementation is modular, supporting data handling, feature engineering, model training, and plotting.

Results & Discussion

The final model tracks stable periods well and captures hourly patterns (R² ≈ 0.986). However, errors increase during volatile periods with sudden spikes.

In class, the project results were presented with visual comparisons (actual vs predicted) and performance discussion. This webpage focuses on clear structure, visuals, and links as required by HW02.

Strength

During stable periods, the model follows demand cycles closely and maintains strong predictive performance.

Limitation

The model struggles with rapidly changing/volatile periods. Future models (LSTM/Transformer) may better capture long-term dependencies and sudden shifts.

Future Work

Integrate deep learning approaches, richer data sources, and deployment-ready inference for BEMS operation and demand response.
